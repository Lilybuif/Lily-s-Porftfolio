Leading a Real-World Design Project: Building the AYSA Youth Sports Website

Ngoc Phan Bao Bui May 7, 2025
CASE STUDY LEADERSHIP UI/UX DESIGN

Introduction

In the design industry, leadership is not determined by titles or years of experience, but rather by the capacity to steer a project through difficult obstacles from start to finish. By redesigning the AYSA Kent Academy youth sports website, I was able to gain a deeper knowledge of this notion. As a student of information technology who is passionate about UI/UX design, this project was my first experience taking on design leadership in the real world. I learned from this experience that good leadership requires a blend of technical expertise, communication skills, emotional intelligence, and problem-solving talents. This essay looks at how the project improved my design skills and gave me priceless knowledge on iterative design methods, stakeholder management, and the value of user-centered solutions.

Assumption of Leadership Responsibilities

I saw a chance to apply what I had learned in the classroom to a worthwhile community initiative when I first came across AYSA's antiquated website. I offered to take the lead on the redesign project even though I lacked official authority and work experience. Making this choice forced me to take on several responsibilities right away, including client liaison, designer, developer, and project manager. I created a project schedule that had distinct checkpoints for the phases of research, design, development, and testing. Obtaining specific requests from stakeholders who lacked digital competence was the biggest initial issue. I assisted them in articulating their primary needs through organized interviews and active listening: an easily navigable platform that mirrored their principles of discipline and inclusivity while streamlining the registration process for working parents.

Navigating the Design Process

Complex problems that put my problem-solving skills to the test were presented during the design phase. In the absence of pre-existing brand guidelines, I carried out visual research to develop a style guide with a sophisticated color scheme (black, white, and cream) and font that struck a balance between professionalism and approachability. Information architecture was especially challenging because the website had to support several sports programs for different age groups. To test navigation flows, I made low-fidelity wireframes and used card sorting techniques to arrange material rationally. I learnt to offer data-driven alternatives rather than flatly rejecting features that stakeholders desired but went against usability best practices. When asked to add a lot of promotional banners, for example, I used heatmap studies to show how this could increase cognitive load and finally suggest a better balanced approach.

Client Communication and Expectation Management

Effective communication emerged as the most crucial aspect of project leadership. I adapted my technical vocabulary to match stakeholders' understanding, using analogies like comparing responsive design to "water fitting differently shaped containers." Regular progress updates with visual prototypes prevented misalignment and built trust. When scope creep threatened timelines, I implemented a phased delivery approach, prioritizing essential functions for initial launch while scheduling enhancements for subsequent updates. This strategy maintained momentum while ensuring quality. Perhaps most importantly, I learned to transform subjective feedback ("make it pop more") into actionable insights by asking probing questions about underlying concerns and desired user emotions.

Technical Implementation and Problem-Solving

The development phase revealed gaps between theoretical knowledge and practical application. Cross-browser compatibility issues and mobile responsiveness challenges required extensive research and experimentation. I implemented progressive enhancement principles to ensure functionality across varying device capabilities in the community. Performance optimization became critical after discovering many users accessed the site through older mobile devices with limited data plans. Techniques like image compression, lazy loading, and CSS minimization improved load times by 40%. These technical hurdles reinforced the importance of adaptability and continuous learning in leadership roles.

Testing and Iterative Improvement

User testing with parents and coaches uncovered unexpected pain points, particularly in the registration workflow. Observing users struggle with multi-step forms led to simplifying the process through chunking and clearer error messaging. In order to foster a culture of continuous development, I set up a feedback loop where stakeholders could report problems after the launch. The concrete impact of user-centered design concepts was demonstrated by the 30% increase in completed registrations that this iterative method produced when compared to the previous system.

Reflection on Leadership Development

My perspective on design leadership has changed as a result of this project. I discovered that genuine leadership is demonstrated by encouraging cooperation amongst all stakeholders rather than by making decisions all at once. The event made clear how crucial emotional intelligence is for striking a balance between user requirements, technological limitations, and corporate objectives. I learned how to be true to the project's goal while still being adaptable to any necessary adjustmentsâ€”a critical skill for any leader. Most importantly, I became more confident in my ability to learn from problems rather than relying on prior information that was flawless.

Conclusion

The AYSA website project was a hands-on design leadership master class. Beyond technical proficiency, it taught me that effective leadership involves established procedures, transparent communication, and the humility to absorb knowledge from stakeholders and users alike. My perspective on design issues and teamwork has been greatly influenced by these teachings. I continue to believe that leadership is about developing frameworks to find answers together rather than having all the answers as I advance in my career. My confidence to take on increasingly challenging projects while being rooted in inclusive design principles and user demands has grown as a result of this experience. A testament to the effectiveness of well-considered, well-led design activities is the website's successful launch and good community impact.
